A real story

What I'm like when
things get hard.

⚙ Internship · Skymeric Technologies · Manufacturing Client
7 systems, 7 PLC machines, 10 days stuck — and a non technical client.

During my internship, I was given a computer vision project for a manufacturing company that wanted to monitor operators via live camera feeds. My role was everything — writing the code, handling client communication, and eventually deploying the application across 7 different systems, each connected to a different PLC machine.

The hardest part was integrating with the PLC machines over Modbus. My teammate had tested signals in a simulator and assumed it was working. I assumed he'd opened the port. Neither of us had confirmed it with each other — and we spent 10 days with signals just not coming through consistently, going in circles.

I went back to basics. I studied the Modbus Python library properly, added structured exception handling, and finally saw a clear error message — port not open. Simple. But when I told the client, they pushed back hard, insisting the fault was on our side.

Instead of arguing, I calmly walked their senior person explaning through the exact error output. They saw it themselves — their port wasn't configured and had security protocols blocking the connection. Once they fixed it, we connected immediately.

After that came a new problem: the camera was getting confused by too many people moving around on the floor. So I modified the algorithm to focus on the operator closest to the camera. Then came more client requirements — face login, cycle tracking, daily report generation with snapshots. I built all of it, packaged everything into an executable with batch files to handle different system configurations across all 7 machines.

Deploying it and seeing it work correctly on every single system — that was one of the most satisfying moments of the whole internship.

"Not just a freelancer — during my internship I was writing code, debugging Modbus protocols, explaining error logs to aggressive clients, and deploying on real hardware. Real world problems, non-technical clients, changing requirements mid-project. I've been there."
